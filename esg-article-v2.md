# Earth Overshoot Day Is Coming. Can One Product Choice Actually Make a Difference?

According to the Global Footprint Network, Earth Overshoot Day 2025 falls on **July 24**. On that day, humanity will have used up all the renewable resources the Earth can regenerate in an entire year.

Let that sink in. From January 1 to July 24, we're living within budget. After July 24, we're borrowing from next year — running an ecological deficit that grows every year.

Right now, we're depleting resources at a rate **80% faster** than the Earth can recover. That means we need 1.8 Earths to sustain our current lifestyle. We only have one.

## The History We Don't Talk About

Earth Overshoot Day isn't a new problem. We crossed the line for the first time in the early 1970s. Back then, we were living roughly within Earth's capacity — overshoot was measured in days, not months.

Since then, the date has crept earlier every decade:

| Decade | Overshoot Day | Trend |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| 1971 | December 25 | Balanced |
| 1981 | November 20 | -35 days |
| 1991 | October 20 | -31 days |
| 2001 | October 4 | -16 days |
| 2011 | August 27 | -38 days |
| 2021 | July 29 | -29 days |
| 2025 | July 24 | -5 days |

The pace is brutal and consistent. At this rate, by 2050 we'll hit overshoot in June.

## Country Overshoot Days: The Map That Tells the Real Story

Here's where it gets uncomfortable.

If every human lived like the average American, Earth Overshoot Day would land in **March**. If everyone lived like the average German, it'd be **early May**. The EU — often praised for strict carbon regulation — still runs a massive ecological deficit.

This reveals something most ESG discussions avoid: **per-capita ecological footprint correlates more strongly with GDP per capita than with regulatory ambition.** Rich countries regulate more, but they also consume more — and the consumption wins.

This doesn't mean regulation is useless. It means **production-based carbon accounting — counting emissions where the factory is, not where the consumer is — systematically hides the true footprint of wealthy nations.** If we switched to consumption-based accounting, most EU countries would see their Overshoot Day move even earlier.

## Why Is China in the Middle?

China is the world's largest carbon emitter by total volume. Yet its country Overshoot Day lands around **late May** — behind the US and most EU nations. Why?

The answer is population. China's 1.4 billion people dilute its per-capita footprint, placing it in the middle of the global ranking. But this number conceals a deeper truth: **China's ecological footprint is overwhelmingly driven by its high- and middle-income households.** The top 10% of Chinese consumers by income have a per-capita footprint comparable to Germany's.

With rising living standards and an expanding middle class, China's total energy demand will continue to grow. The emergency isn't "China as a whole" — it's the consumption patterns of its wealthiest citizens. Reducing the ecological footprint of those groups should be the priority.

### How to Calculate a Country's Overshoot Day

For the curious, the math is straightforward:

**Step 1:** Calculate how many days Earth's resources would last if everyone lived like that country's average citizen:

```
Days = 365 ÷ (per-capita ecological footprint ÷ global per-capita biocapacity)
```

**Step 2:** Count forward from January 1.

*Example: China*
- Per-capita ecological footprint ≈ 3.4 global hectares (gha)
- Global per-capita biocapacity ≈ 1.6 gha
- Days = 365 ÷ (3.4 ÷ 1.6) ≈ 172
- 172nd day of 2025 → **June 21**

That means if every human lived like the average Chinese, overshoot would still arrive nearly two months ahead of schedule. Sustainable? No. But it's also nowhere near the American or European level.

## The Gap No One Bridges: Systems vs. Choices

At this point, most ESG articles pivot to policy. Carbon pricing. Renewable infrastructure. Circular economy mandates. All necessary. All system-level.

But there's a question that rarely gets asked: **what about the stuff we buy?**

Every product on your desk — including the device you're reading this on — carries an ecological debt. Raw materials extracted somewhere. Energy burned somewhere. Chemicals released somewhere. That debt accumulates across millions of individual purchases, and collectively, it's what pushes Overshoot Day earlier each year.

This isn't about consumer-shaming. It's about **transparency**. If we can't see the true environmental cost of a single product, how can we claim to understand the system?

Let's test this. Let's take one "sustainable" product and hold it up to the data.

# Case Study: The Heyfold Eternal Notebook

**Product claim:** A reusable silicone notebook that lasts 500 years, eliminating paper waste.

**The question:** Is this a genuine sustainability innovation — or green marketing dressed in good intentions?

## Materials and Manufacturing

The notebook is made from **Permaflex silicone**, a material chosen for durability. Silicone doesn't tear like paper. It can be written on and erased thousands of times.

But here's what the marketing doesn't mention:

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Reusable for years, potentially replacing dozens of paper notebooks | Silicone is derived from silica through high-temperature processing — significant CO₂ output |
| Reduces paper waste at the consumer end | Not biodegradable. Not widely recyclable in municipal systems |
| Durable — won't degrade in normal use | Manufacturing involves chemical processing with its own toxic effluent streams |

The trade-off is real: you're swapping a renewable, biodegradable material (paper) for a durable, non-biodegradable one (silicone). Whether that's a net environmental win depends entirely on **how long you use it**.

## The "500-Year" Claim

This is where the marketing gets ahead of the evidence.

Heyfold claims the notebook lasts 500 years. But no independent accelerated aging test has been published. No third-party durability certification exists. The writing surface and coating will almost certainly degrade long before the silicone substrate does.

**"500 years" is not a data point — it's a storytelling device.** It sounds impressive precisely because it's unfalsifiable. No reviewer will be alive in 500 years to dispute it.

This is a textbook greenwashing pattern: a claim so distant in time that it bypasses scrutiny. The product may well be durable. But "500 years" belongs in a fantasy novel, not an ESG evaluation.

A more honest claim: if you use this notebook for 5–10 years (a realistic estimate for the writing surface), it could replace roughly 10–20 paper notebooks. That's a meaningful reduction — but it requires a genuine long-term commitment from the user, not just a purchase.

## ESG Evaluation

### E — Environmental

**Strengths:**
- Reusable design theoretically reduces paper consumption
- Promotes a low-waste mindset for students and professionals

**Weaknesses:**
- Silicone production is energy-intensive and carbon-emitting
- **No lifecycle analysis (LCA) has been published** — carbon footprint, water footprint, and end-of-life pathways are unknown
- No third-party environmental certification (Cradle to Cradle, ISO 14001, or FSC for any paper components)
- Packaging sustainability claims lack detail

**Verdict:** Good intentions, zero transparency. A product sold on environmental promises owes its buyers a published LCA. Without one, the "sustainable" label is just a label.

### S — Social

**Strengths:**
- Encourages mindful consumption habits
- Combines analog creativity with digital storage — a genuine productivity angle

**Weaknesses:**
- Priced for a design-conscious, affluent market segment
- At an estimated $30–50, it costs the equivalent of roughly **200 paper notebooks in China**
- Raises equity concerns: is "sustainability" becoming a luxury good?

**Verdict:** Positive behavioral impact for those who can afford it. But the product reinforces a pattern where sustainable consumption is reserved for the wealthy — exactly the dynamic that drives overshoot in the first place.

### G — Governance

**Weaknesses:**
- Heyfold is a startup with minimal public disclosure on supply chain, manufacturing location, or corporate ESG practices
- No third-party environmental certifications
- Vague sustainability claims without audited evidence

**Verdict:** Weak governance. Early-stage companies don't need perfect ESG infrastructure, but they do need honesty about what they don't yet know. Heyfold's marketing currently overstates its maturity.

## The Cost Comparison: When Does "Reusable" Actually Win?

| Option | Est. Carbon Footprint | Lifespan | Annualized Cost (CO₂) |
|--------|----------------------|----------|----------------------|
| Paper notebook (1/year) | ~1.5 kg CO₂eq each | 1 year | ~1.5 kg/yr |
| Heyfold Eternal Notebook | ~25–35 kg CO₂eq (estimated, no LCA) | 5–10 years (realistic) | ~2.5–7 kg/yr |
| iPad + Apple Pencil | ~80 kg CO₂eq | 5 years | ~16 kg/yr |

*Paper notebook data from Environmental Paper Network (2021). Heyfold estimate is a rough approximation based on silicone LCA literature — the company has not published its own numbers.*

The math is clear: **a Heyfold notebook needs to replace 15–20 paper notebooks to break even on carbon.** For a heavy note-taker who goes through 2–3 notebooks per year, this is achievable in 5–7 years. For a casual user who fills one notebook every two years, the carbon math never closes — the manufacturing debt is too high.

This doesn't make the product bad. It makes the product **conditional**. It works for some people and not others. That nuance is exactly what green marketing erases.

## The Deeper Problem: "Buy Your Way to Sustainability"

The Heyfold case reveals a pattern that extends far beyond notebooks.

A well-off consumer buys a $40 silicone notebook, feels good about reducing waste, and stops there. But their total ecological footprint — driven by housing size, transportation, diet, and overall consumption volume — dwarfs whatever marginal benefit the notebook provides.

This is the **"green consumption trap":** using sustainable products as a moral offset for unsustainable lifestyles. It's particularly relevant to China's high-income households, which — as we established above — are the primary driver of the country's ecological overshoot.

The trap works because it's invisible. No one hands you a statement at checkout showing how your notebook purchase compares to your flight last month. Without that visibility, sustainable consumption becomes a vibe, not a metric.

## Where This Leaves Us

Earth Overshoot Day will keep moving earlier unless two things happen simultaneously:

1. **System-level change** — decarbonized grids, circular supply chains, consumption-based carbon accounting
2. **Individual-level transparency** — every product carrying a quantified environmental cost, not a green label

My project, [BatteryTrace](https://batterytrace.org), is an attempt at the second piece. It calculates the carbon footprint, water footprint, ecotoxicity, and recyclability of power banks — consumer electronics that most people replace every 2–3 years without ever knowing their environmental cost. The data isn't perfect (it's based on published LCA literature, not factory measurements), but it's transparent about its limitations. That's more than most "sustainable" products offer.

The question isn't whether you should buy the silicone notebook or the paper one. It's whether you have the data to make that choice honestly.

Right now, you don't. And until we demand that data — from Heyfold, from Apple, from every brand that prints "sustainable" on a box — Overshoot Day will keep creeping toward January.
